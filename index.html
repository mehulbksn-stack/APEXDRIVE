Ferrari SF90 Stradale: The Hybrid That Bites

By Deepesh R.  ·  June 12, 2025  ·  8 min read

There is a moment, somewhere around 160 km/h on a private airstrip outside Maranello, when the Ferrari SF90 Stradale stops feeling like a car and starts feeling like a philosophical argument. You are being propelled by 1,000 horsepower — 769 from a twin-turbocharged V8 and an additional 220 from three electric motors.

"Ferrari didn't build a hybrid. They built a weapon and then gave it a conscience."

The Powertrain: Science Fiction Made Steel

The SF90 uses Ferrari's PHEV setup. The internal combustion engine drives the rear wheels through an 8-speed dual-clutch gearbox, while two electric motors power the front axle independently — making it Ferrari's first all-wheel-drive road car. A third electric motor sits between the engine and gearbox, acting as torque-fill and starter motor.

On electric power alone, the SF90 can travel up to 25 km at speeds up to 135 km/h.

On the Road: Fast Doesn't Cover It

The 0–100 km/h sprint takes 2.5 seconds — alongside the Bugatti Chiron and Rimac Nevera. Yet it feels different from both: more aggressive on turn-in than the Bugatti, more mechanical in feedback than the all-electric Rimac.

For a deeper comparison with rival hypercars, see Top Gear's hypercar feature.

Should You Buy One?

At roughly ₹7.5 crore ex-showroom, the SF90 is not a rational purchase. But it is Ferrari's statement that electrification does not mean the death of driving — possibly the most important supercar of this decade.

Is the Internal Combustion Engine Truly Dead?

By Deepesh R.  ·  May 28, 2025  ·  6 min read

Every few months, a new headline announces the death of the internal combustion engine. The EU wants to ban new petrol and diesel cars by 2035. California follows. China electrifies at pace. And yet — the ICE refuses to die quietly. Global car sales in 2024 showed EVs still account for less than 20% of new vehicles sold worldwide.

"The ICE isn't dying. It's being slowly, deliberately, and somewhat unfairly defunded."

The Case Against the Eulogy

Internal combustion engines have improved dramatically. Modern turbocharged units produce less CO₂ per kilometer than equivalents from 2005. A 2025 Honda Civic's 1.5-litre turbo is arguably more sophisticated than some early EV powertrains.

Furthermore, in countries where the grid is coal-heavy — including large parts of India, where the IEA estimates coal still generates over 70% of electricity — an EV charged overnight may have a larger carbon footprint than a modern petrol hybrid.

The Emotional Argument

There is something data doesn't capture: the sound, the smell, the tactile experience. The howl of a naturally aspirated V8 at 8,000 RPM is information, drama, and joy. Electric cars are fast — many are brilliant — but they are emotionally quieter.

The ICE will recede into niches, and in those niches it will be cherished. Like vinyl records, it may survive precisely because people love it too much to let go.
